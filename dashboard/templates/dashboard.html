Geopolitical trends
{{ charts.geo_us_vs_china_overall | safe }}
The United States maintained dominant patent activity from 1990 through 2018, reflecting decades of accumulated R&D infrastructure, corporate innovation pipelines, and strong intellectual property incentives. China's steady upward trajectory from near-zero in 1990 to over 100,000 patents by 2022 reflects the deliberate transformation of its economy from manufacturing-led growth toward technology-intensive innovation, accelerated by state policy initiatives including WTO accession in 2001 and the Made in China 2025 strategy launched in 2015.
{{ charts.geo_korea_vs_japan_h01l | safe }}
Japan held a commanding lead in semiconductor patents through the 1980s and 1990s, driven by its dominance in memory chip manufacturing and consumer electronics. Korea's steady rise from the mid-1990s onwards reflects the strategic investments of Samsung and SK Hynix in DRAM and NAND flash technologies, which progressively displaced Japanese firms from global market leadership.
{{ charts.geo_trade_war_semiconductors | safe }}
Within the reliable data range of 2010 to 2019, the United States maintained the largest share of semiconductor patents, reflecting the concentration of fabless chip design firms including Qualcomm, Intel, and Nvidia. Taiwan's sustained growth reflects TSMC's expanding role as the dominant global contract manufacturer. China's gradual increase through 2018 followed by apparent flattening aligns with the imposition of US export controls and entity list designations beginning in 2018, which restricted Chinese firms access to advanced semiconductor tooling and design software, a geopolitical intervention whose full effect on Chinese domestic patent strategy extends beyond this dataset's reliable range.

Evolution of Patent Activity: IBM and Samsung
{{ charts.ibm_vs_samsung | safe }}
The trends indicate that IBM initially dominated patent filings due to its strong focus on enterprise computing and research-driven innovation, but its decline after the late 2000s suggests a strategic shift toward software, cloud services, and selective high-value patents. In contrast, Samsung experienced rapid patent growth as a result of heavy investment in smartphones, semiconductors, and consumer electronics, overtaking IBM during the peak mobile technology expansion period before both companies experienced declining filings due to market maturity and more optimized patent strategies.

Technology trends
{{ charts.diag_smartphones_vs_telephony | safe }}
Wireless communication patents rapidly overtook traditional telephony after the smartphone era began, reflecting the global shift from fixed voice infrastructure toward mobile internet, apps, and data-driven communication ecosystems.
{{ charts.diag_ev_vs_combustion | safe }}
Combustion engine patents dominated for decades due to established automotive infrastructure, but EV patents surged after 2010 as environmental regulation, battery advances, and electrification reshaped the automotive industry.
{{ charts.diag_ai_vs_software | safe }}
Traditional software patents remained dominant because conventional computing underpinned the digital economy for decades, but AI patents accelerated sharply as machine learning became commercially viable through advances in data, computing power, and automation demand.
{{ charts.trend_streaming_vs_optical | safe }}
Optical disc patents peaked around 2008, the same year Netflix launched streaming, and never recovered. Streaming patents then rode a decade of growth fueled by codec innovation, CDN infrastructure and DRM development before dipping post-2020 as the market consolidated and fewer new players meant fewer filings. A textbook disruption curve where the incumbent peaks exactly as the challenger reaches escape velocity.
{{ charts.trend_renewables_vs_fossil | safe }}
Fossil fuel patents grew through the shale and deepwater drilling boom then fell sharply when oil prices crashed in 2014 and gutted R&D budgets. Renewables grew slowly but never stopped, kept alive by policy incentives even through cheap oil periods. The gap is still large but fossil fuels are declining and renewables are the only category still climbing. Patent counts also lag real investment by a few years so the renewable momentum is even stronger than the chart suggests.
{{ charts.trend_battery_vs_oil | safe }}
Battery patents exploded through 2018 as EV makers and grid storage companies raced to lock down cell chemistry and pack architecture, then dropped sharply. That spike and crash is a classic sign of a technology crossing from lab to mass production where the key claims get filed fast and then the thicket matures. Oil patents declined on the same post-2014 trajectory as fossil fuel extraction. The two curves are now converging, meaning both industries are innovating at roughly the same intensity for the first time.
